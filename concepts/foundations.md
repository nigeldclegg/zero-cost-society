# Foundations

**Domain:** foundations
**Status:** developing

## What it is

The base argument this whole project rests on: that money is a tool invented to manage scarcity, that scarcity in the essentials is now largely an engineering problem rather than a physical fact, and that a society could therefore organise the essentials without money at its centre — not as sacrifice, but as improvement.

This page sets out that argument, the conditions a moneyless community appears to require, and the strongest objections to both.

## Why the current system prevents it

Money is not free. Every pound made is made from something and taken from somewhere, and the ledger the world keeps shows only one side of the entry. The cost side lands on people, on land, on water, on time, and on the future, and it is not counted because nothing in the system requires it to be.

This is not a claim about greed. It is a claim about accounting. A system that measures only one side of an exchange will systematically produce outcomes that look profitable and are not. Exhausted soil, dead rivers, products designed to fail, work that produces nothing anyone needs, and people who are hungry where there is food and lonely where there are people everywhere — these are not malfunctions. They are what the measurement produces when it is working correctly.

The specific mechanism that blocks alternatives: anything that reduces the amount of money moving through a system is, by that system's own measure, a failure. A community that grows its own food, repairs its own goods, and needs very little has done something the ledger cannot record as success. So no institution inside the system is incentivised to build one, fund one, or notice one working.

## How it works

### Context

The argument applies to essentials — food, energy, shelter, water, transport, care, knowledge. It does not claim every human want can be made abundant, and does not need to. It claims that where the essentials are secure, most of what money is currently used to coordinate stops needing coordination by price.

It applies at community scale first. Nothing here requires a nation to change its mind before anything can begin.

### What to look for

Three changes make the argument possible now, none of which were true in 1970:

**Food.** Controlled-environment and intensive growing methods produce far more per square metre than field agriculture, year-round, close to where people live.

**Energy.** Solar and wind are now the cheapest new generation in most of the world, and still falling. Energy at near-zero marginal cost changes what is possible downstream of it.

**Making.** Automated production makes most physical goods at very low marginal cost. What remains expensive is largely design, distribution, marketing and profit — not manufacture.

Where all three hold, the physical basis for essential scarcity is substantially gone. What remains is an allocation problem, not a production problem.

### What the variations mean

The conditions below appear to be what a working moneyless community requires. They are drawn from observing what makes such attempts succeed or fail, and are offered as a working hypothesis, not a law.

**1. Land** — adequate, appropriate, and securely held by the community rather than rented or tolerated.

**2. Infrastructure** — the initial material investment: growing structures, energy, water, buildings. This is the one condition that can be straightforwardly supplied from outside.

**3. Knowledge** — practical, accumulated, local. The binding constraint in almost every case. It cannot be bought, only grown, and it moves mainly through direct contact.

**4. Time** — protection from the pressure to show results on someone else's schedule. The condition most often destroyed by well-intentioned funding, because timelines force the visible over the foundational.

**5. Connection** — between communities. Isolated ones are fragile. Networked ones share knowledge, seed, surplus and repair, and become robust.

**6. Removal of the money logic from the operating system** — not the removal of resources, but the removal of price and profit as the mechanism that decides who gets what *inside* the community. This is the condition that produces the others' effects, and the one that fails if reintroduced in any form, however kind.

### Procedure

There is no procedure for a society. There is a procedure for a community, and it is roughly: secure the land, install enough infrastructure to feed and warm people, spend the first years building knowledge rather than showing results, connect to others doing the same, and never let price back in as the internal mechanism.

Each domain page in this repository covers one part of that in detail.

### Common failures

**Funding with strings.** Money arriving with reporting requirements and deadlines destroys condition four, which quietly destroys the rest.

**Copying the form.** Building the towers, the systems and the buildings without growing the knowledge produces a fragile imitation that fails at the first unexpected condition.

**Reintroducing price internally.** Usually as a fairness measure — credits, hours, contribution tracking. It reliably restores the behaviour it was meant to prevent.

**Isolation.** A single community with no network has no redundancy and no fresh knowledge, and tends to calcify or fail.

## What cannot be written down

Most of it, honestly.

The knowledge that makes these places work is practical and accumulated: reading soil, knowing what a plant needs before instruments say so, sensing when a group decision is going wrong. It transfers through working alongside someone, over seasons. Documentation shortens the path. It does not replace it.

A reasonable estimate from those doing it is that written material carries perhaps half to two-thirds of what is needed, and the rest arrives only through contact and time. Any claim that this repository could be enough to build from is false, and should be treated as a warning sign wherever it appears.

## Evidence

**Partial, and honestly so.** No large-scale modern society has run without money. Claims that this is proven are overreach.

What does exist:

- **Non-monetary economies at scale.** Substantial historical and anthropological evidence that societies coordinated production and distribution for millennia without currency or barter, largely through obligation and reciprocity. See David Graeber, *Debt: The First 5000 Years*.
- **Commons management.** Elinor Ostrom's Nobel-recognised work documents long-lived shared-resource systems governed without private ownership or state control, and identifies the design principles that make them last.
- **Intentional communities.** Kibbutzim, and numerous religious and secular communities, have run internal economies without money for decades — with well-documented successes and failures worth studying closely.
- **Open source.** Linux and its ecosystem demonstrate that enormously valuable, complex production can be organised without payment or ownership, and can outcompete proprietary alternatives. Directly relevant to knowledge and making.
- **Cost curves.** The falling costs of solar, wind and storage are documented in public data from the IEA, IRENA and Our World in Data, and are the strongest empirical leg of the argument.

Contributions strengthening this section — or honestly weakening it — are among the most useful things anyone can add.

## Unsolved

**Scale.** Everything here is demonstrated at community scale or in single domains. How a network of communities coordinates across millions of people is not solved, and hand-waving it is the most common weakness in this kind of thinking.

**Transition.** How you get from here to there without collapse, coercion or a decade of chaos. Largely unaddressed in this repository so far.

**Interface with the money world.** A community still needs things it cannot make, from suppliers who want paying. Every real attempt runs a boundary economy. How that works without the money logic leaking inward is genuinely unresolved.

**Scarce and unevenly distributed goods.** Some things are genuinely limited: rare minerals, certain medicines, particular land. Allocation without price is hard here and the honest answer is that it is not worked out.

**Population and mobility.** What happens when far more people want to join a community than it can support. Whether refusal is compatible with the principle.

## Objections

**"This has been tried and it failed."**
Large-scale command economies failed, and the failures were catastrophic. This project is not proposing central planning by a state — that is a different thing with a different failure mode, and any version of this that ends in a central authority allocating by decree should be opposed. But the objection lands in one respect: the burden of proof is on this argument, and it has not been met at scale.

**"People won't work without payment."**

Contradicted by the evidence, though not settled by it. People do enormous amounts of skilled, difficult work for no payment — open source, care, voluntary work, everything anyone does at home. What is true is that people will not do *degrading, pointless work* without payment, and a system without money would have to make most such work unnecessary or shared, which is a real design requirement, not a rhetorical answer.

**"Someone will free-ride."**
Yes. Every such community reports it, at manageable levels, usually handled socially rather than by rule. The stronger form of this objection is that free-riding scales badly beyond the size where everyone knows everyone, and that is a genuine unsolved problem.

**"Without prices you can't know what to produce."**
The strongest economic objection — Hayek's knowledge problem, and it deserves respect. Prices aggregate dispersed information that no planner can gather. The response is that the informational function of prices can now be performed directly: measuring what is actually needed and available, continuously, openly, without the distortions price introduces. This is a claim about modern information systems, and it is not proven. See `allocation.md`.

**"Small communities work; societies don't."**
Currently unanswerable with evidence. The honest position is that this is the central open question of the project.

**"This is utopian."**
Sometimes used as an argument and sometimes as a dismissal. As an argument it deserves an answer: nothing here requires people to be better than they are, only for the accounting to change. As a dismissal it is not an argument at all.
