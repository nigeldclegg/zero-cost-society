# Making & Automation

**Domain:** making
**Status:** developing

## What it is

Producing physical things without money, and deciding what machines should do and what people should do.

The principle underneath it: **machines take the repetition, people take the judgement.** A machine makes a panel perfectly and a chair only adequately. Give the repetition to the machine and the judgement to the person, and both do what they are good at.

This is also the domain where this project is most obviously incomplete. A community can grow its own food and generate its own power. It cannot currently make its own solar panels, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest.

## Why the current system prevents it

**Things are designed to be replaced, not repaired.** Glued rather than screwed, sealed rather than serviceable, with parts unavailable and diagnostics locked. This is not carelessness — a product that lasts thirty years is a customer lost for twenty-nine.

**Price sets quality, and price is set by what can be extracted.** "Made to a budget" is the ordinary condition of nearly everything, and the budget is set by what the market will bear, not by what the object needs to be good.

**Automation is deployed to reduce wage cost, not to relieve people of drudgery.** The same technology could do either. It does the first because that is what the accounting rewards, which is why automation arrives as a threat rather than a relief.

**Repair is priced out deliberately.** Where the repair costs more than replacement, the object is disposable by design, and the calculation is arranged that way on purpose.

**Enormous production capacity makes things nobody needs**, because the capacity must be kept busy to be justified, and marketing exists to create the demand that justifies it.

## How it works

### Context

Applies to what a community can realistically make: buildings, furniture, tools, textiles, food processing, repair of nearly everything, and simple mechanical and electrical goods. Does not apply to semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, or anything requiring industrial-scale precision. That boundary is real and this page does not pretend to cross it.

### What to look for

**The repetition-judgement split.** Identify, for each process, which parts are identical every time and which require someone to decide. Automate the first without hesitation. Leave the second alone, and resist the argument that it too could be automated at ninety per cent quality.

**Design for a long life and for repair.** Screws not glue. Standard fasteners. Parts that can be replaced individually. Documentation that stays with the object. A thing built so that someone in a hundred years can maintain it is a different category of object from one built to a price.

**Local repair before local manufacture.** A community that can fix things has already solved most of its material problem. Making things from scratch is harder, less urgent, and often unnecessary.

**Shared tools, not owned tools.** Most tools are used for minutes and owned for years. A well-equipped workshop shared by a community outperforms every household owning a poor version of the same thing.

**Materials that can be worked locally.** Timber, stone, clay, wool, steel stock. Composites, laminates and bonded assemblies cannot be repaired or reused, and everything made from them becomes waste at end of life.

### What the variations mean

**"Made to a budget" is visible in the object** once you know how to look — the panel at the lower edge of tolerance, the joint that will fail first, the part that cannot be reached. Learning to see it changes what people accept.

**Automating the judgement produces adequacy at scale.** A machine can make a chair. It makes an adequate chair, identically, forever. The reason to leave chairs to people is not sentiment; it is that the last ten per cent is where the object stops being merely acceptable.

**Automating the chores produces relief.** Nobody's life is improved by hauling bins, mixing nutrient solution to a fixed formula, or performing the same weld four hundred times. Removing that work is straightforwardly good.

**The line moves and should be argued about openly.** What counts as repetition and what counts as judgement is not fixed, and a community that stops asking will drift toward automating everything, which is the failure this principle exists to prevent.

### Procedure

1. **Set up repair first.** A workshop, tools, and people who know how to use them, before any thought of manufacture.
2. **Audit what the community actually needs made**, as opposed to what it currently buys.
3. **Split every process into repetition and judgement**, explicitly, and record the reasoning.
4. **Automate the repetition** with whatever machinery is available, and share it.
5. **Build to last and to be repaired**, and write the documentation as you go.
6. **Be honest about the boundary.** Some things will be bought from the outside economy indefinitely. Record what they are and stop pretending otherwise.

### Common failures

**Automating for its own sake.** Machinery bought because it is impressive, used rarely, maintained by nobody.

**Skipping documentation.** The machine works and one person understands it, which means it works until that person leaves.

**Making what can easily be bought while failing to repair what is already owned.** Novelty over usefulness.

**Automating the interesting part.** People automate what is technically satisfying rather than what is tedious, which produces communities where the machines do the pleasant work.

**Pretending the boundary is not there.** Communities that claim material independence while quietly buying panels, electronics and machinery from the money economy lose credibility with everyone who looks closely, and deservedly.

## What cannot be written down

The hand.

Knowing when a joint is right, when a weld has taken, when timber is going to move. Sanding until the surface goes quiet. Judging by feel what no gauge measures. This is the "judgement" half of the principle and it is not documentable — it is why the split exists in the first place.

It transfers by working next to someone who has it, over years. A community that automates before that knowledge exists locally will find it never develops, because there is nothing left to learn it on.

## Evidence

**Open source hardware.** RepRap, Open Source Ecology, Farm Hack and WikiHouse demonstrate that machine designs, building systems and agricultural equipment can be developed collaboratively, published freely, and built locally. The results are real, working, and generally cruder than commercial equivalents.

**Repair movements.** Repair cafés and the right-to-repair campaigns document both the demand and the deliberate obstruction — locked diagnostics, unavailable parts, and serialised components.

**Planned obsolescence** is documented historically (the Phoebus lightbulb cartel is the standard case) and continues in modified forms, several of which have resulted in regulatory action.

**Distributed manufacturing.** Small-scale CNC, additive manufacturing and laser cutting now put genuine production capability in the reach of a workshop, for a defined and limited class of objects.

**Longevity as a design choice.** Traditional building and furniture-making demonstrate objects functioning for centuries where they were designed to be maintained. Not nostalgia — a different specification.

## Unsolved

**The industrial base.** This is the central unsolved problem of the whole project, not just this page. Semiconductors, batteries, solar panels, medical equipment and precision machinery require global supply chains, extreme capital and industrial scale. No community can make them. Every community described here depends on them. There is no answer to this in this repository and anyone claiming one should be read sceptically.

**Where the machines come from.** The automation that relieves people of drudgery is itself manufactured by the system this project proposes to leave.

**Rare and processed materials.** Metals, plastics and refined chemicals arrive through the money economy, and any account of their true cost implicates every community using them.

**Scale of specialisation.** Some skills need a population far larger than a community to sustain — you cannot maintain a specialist in something needed once a decade. Networks help and probably do not fully solve it.

**End of life.** What happens to complex objects that cannot be repaired locally, when they finally fail. See the unwritten waste page.

## Objections

**"You can't make anything modern without global industry."**
Correct, and this page says so. The claim is narrower: a community can make and maintain far more than it currently does, can stop the deliberate waste, and can be honest about the remainder. That is a real improvement and it is not independence.

**"Automation destroys work, and people need work."**
People need purpose, security and a place in the world, and under the present system employment is the only mechanism supplying all three, which is why automation arrives as a threat. Remove the link between livelihood and employment and removing drudgery becomes straightforwardly good. That response is only available after the transition. During it, the objection stands and is serious.

**"Handmade means worse and slower."**
Often true, which is exactly why the split exists. Nothing here proposes handmade panels or hand-woven cloth. It proposes machines for the identical and people for the judged, and being honest about which is which.

**"Building things to last is a luxury."**
Under current accounting it is — a durable object costs more upfront and the buyer may not be able to afford it. Remove price and the calculation inverts entirely: the only cost that matters is materials and effort, and building once instead of five times is obviously cheaper.

**"Communities will just buy cheap goods from outside."**
Many will, and this is the leak through which the money logic returns. Nothing in this page prevents it. It is a genuine weakness and it belongs in the unsolved list as much as in the objections.
