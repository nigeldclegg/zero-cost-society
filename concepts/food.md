# Food & Growing

**Domain:** food
**Status:** developing

## What it is

Producing enough food for a community, close to where it is eaten, without money organising any part of it.

Not subsistence farming, and not a return to anything. A combination of intensive controlled-environment growing, outdoor polyculture, and seed keeping, run by people who know the specific ground they are standing on.

## Why the current system prevents it

Food is currently produced where land is cheapest, moved to where money is, and priced by what it can extract rather than what it costs to make. The consequences follow logically.

**Varieties are chosen for transport and shelf life, not nutrition or flavour.** A tomato bred to survive a lorry is a different object from one bred to be eaten.

**Soil is treated as a substrate rather than a living system**, because building soil takes years and shows on no quarterly account. Depleting it shows on none either, until it does.

**Enormous quantities are thrown away** — roughly a third globally — while people go hungry, because the food is where the money isn't. This is not a distribution failure. It is distribution working exactly as designed.

**Thousands of varieties have been lost**, not because they were inferior but because they were less convenient to sell. Seed catalogues that once listed thousands now list dozens.

**Knowledge has been deliberately narrowed.** A farmer who buys seed, fertiliser and advice from the same supplier has been relieved of the need to know things their grandparents knew, which is efficient for the supplier and fragile for everyone.

## How it works

### Context

Applies where a community has secure land, some capital for initial structures, and — critically — several years before anyone expects results.

Suits temperate maritime climates well (the evidence base here is largely northern European). Hot arid and tropical contexts need substantially different methods and are underrepresented in this repository. Contributions especially welcome.

### What to look for

The system has three parts that support each other.

**Controlled-environment growing.** Vertical or stacked growing under glass or panels, climate-managed, water recirculating. High yield per square metre, year-round, low water use. Best for leaves, herbs, salad crops, seedlings and anything that benefits from consistency.

**Outdoor polyculture.** Mixed plantings in real soil, outdoors. Lower yield per square metre, far lower input, and it builds rather than consumes the growing medium. Best for staples, roots, brassicas, legumes, and anything that wants weather and soil biology.

**Seed keeping.** Saving, testing, growing out and redistributing seed from what you grow. This is what makes the system independent rather than dependent, and it is the part most often skipped.

The three are not alternatives. The towers feed people through winter; the outdoor beds build the soil and grow the bulk; the seed library makes both repeatable without buying anything.

### What the variations mean

**Reading plants before instruments.** Colour, leaf set, rate of new growth, the difference between healthy and merely not-diseased. Sensors report what someone who knew taught them to look for; the eye catches what no one thought to measure.

**Soil biology as the actual crop.** In restored ground, the mycorrhizal network — fine fungal threads connecting root systems — is what makes everything else possible. It moves water and nutrients between plants and carries chemical signals. You cannot build it quickly. You give it carbon, cover and minimal disturbance, and it builds itself at whatever rate it builds.

**Drift and its causes.** Most problems in controlled systems are not where they show. A nutrient loss visible at one stage often begins at an earlier transition. Measuring downstream of the event is the commonest diagnostic error.

**Germination rates as a health indicator.** A saved variety's germination percentage tells you whether the seed line is alive, dying, or recovering. Old seed kept in a drawer for decades can drop below half. Grown out carefully over several seasons, it recovers.

### Procedure

Roughly, and in order:

1. **Assess the ground honestly.** Contaminated, compacted or dead soil is common on post-industrial land and is recoverable, slowly.
2. **Build enough controlled growing to feed people through the first winters.** This is what buys the time for everything else.
3. **Start the outdoor beds immediately**, expecting little from them for two or three years. Cover, carbon, no disturbance.
4. **Start saving seed from year one**, even badly. The library compounds.
5. **Attach a learner to every task.** Knowledge kept in one head is a single point of failure, and the community's resilience is the number of people who can do each thing.
6. **Connect to other communities** and exchange seed, varieties and methods early.

### Common failures

**Expecting outdoor yields in year one.** The soil is not ready. Communities that plan around early outdoor harvests go hungry and lose faith.

**Skipping seed saving** because bought seed is easy. Three years later the community is dependent on a supplier and has lost the local adaptation it was accidentally breeding.

**Automating the growing rather than the chores.** Pumps, dosing, climate and waste handling should be automated — nobody's life improves by hauling bins. The growing itself is where the knowledge lives, and a system nobody touches is a system nobody understands when it goes wrong.

**Monoculture inside the polytunnel.** Concentrated growing concentrates pests and disease. Mixed plantings are less efficient per square metre and far more robust.

**One expert.** If a single person holds the knowledge, the system fails when they leave, fall ill or burn out.

## What cannot be written down

The largest gap of any domain here.

You cannot learn soil from a page. Knowing what a bed needs by looking at it, catching a problem a week before instruments do, understanding how your particular ground behaves in a wet spring — all of it comes from seasons of hands in that specific ground.

Written material can carry what to look for, what the readings mean, the procedures, and the common failures. It cannot carry the judgement. Realistically, perhaps half of what makes a grower good. The rest arrives by working beside someone who already has it, over years.

This is why the knowledge condition is the binding constraint, and why communities are built by contact rather than instruction.

## Evidence

**Yields.** Controlled-environment agriculture reliably produces many times the yield per square metre of field agriculture for suitable crops, year-round, using a fraction of the water. Well documented commercially — the technology is proven and unexceptional. What is not commercially demonstrated is running it without profit as the organising principle.

**Soil restoration.** Regenerative and no-dig methods rebuilding degraded soil are well evidenced. See the Rodale Institute's long-running Farming Systems Trial, and the extensive literature on mycorrhizal networks (Suzanne Simard's work is the accessible entry point).

**Seed conservation.** Seed Savers Exchange, the Heritage Seed Library, and Svalbard demonstrate both the scale of variety loss and that recovery of near-dead seed lines is achievable.

**Waste.** FAO estimates roughly a third of food produced globally is lost or wasted — the clearest evidence that hunger is an allocation problem rather than a production one.

**Community-scale food production.** Cuba's post-1989 urban agriculture transition is the largest real-world case of a country rapidly reorganising food production under resource collapse, with genuinely mixed results worth studying properly rather than citing selectively.

## Unsolved

**Staples and calories.** Leaves and salad crops are easy. Grain, oil and protein at community scale are hard, land-hungry, and largely unaddressed here. Most community food projects quietly buy their carbohydrates.

**Labour.** How many people does feeding a community actually take, without money to compel participation and with automation limited to chores? Estimates vary wildly and almost none are rigorous.

**Hot and arid climates.** The methods described here are temperate-biased. Water-scarce contexts need a different page, written by someone who has done it.

**Livestock.** Deliberately unaddressed. Communities differ on whether animals feature at all, and the arguments are unresolved. Someone should write this page.

**Failure years.** Weather, disease, a bad season. A community with no money cannot simply buy its way through a crop failure. Network redundancy is the proposed answer and it is untested at scale.

## Objections

**"This is just gardening, and gardening can't feed a country."**
Correct as stated, and the honest answer is that nobody has demonstrated feeding a country this way. The claim is narrower: a community can feed itself, and a network of communities can cover each other's shortfalls. Whether that composes upward to national scale is genuinely unproven.

**"Controlled-environment growing is energy-intensive."**
True, and a real cost. The response is that where energy is renewable and near-zero marginal cost, the objection weakens considerably — but it only weakens, it does not vanish, and a community running towers on a grid powered by gas has not solved the problem it thinks it has.

**"Yields per square metre 

A fair technical objection. Land is not usually the binding constraint in food production; labour, water and energy often are. This page's emphasis on yield density reflects the fact that community land is genuinely limited, but the objection stands.

**"You'd be poorer and eat worse."**
Contradicted by everyone who has done it, who report the opposite: food grown that morning and eaten that day is better than anything in a supermarket. But this is testimony, not evidence, and it is testimony from self-selecting people who chose this life.

**"Without a market, how do you know how much to grow?"**
Ask people. At community scale this is not the hard problem it is at national scale — the number of people is known, what they eat is known, and adjustment is annual and observable. It becomes hard at scale, which is `allocation.md`'s problem.
