# The format

Every concept page follows the same seven-part shape. It exists so that pages can be compared, combined and translated, and so that no page can quietly skip the hard parts.

Ordinary readers should be able to read a page top to bottom and understand it. Practitioners should be able to read the middle sections and use it.

---

## The seven parts

### 1. What it is
Two or three sentences in plain language. What is the thing being described? A person who reads only this should come away with an accurate picture.

### 2. Why the current system prevents it
What specifically stops this now? Be precise: not "capitalism", but the actual mechanism. Who pays, who profits, what incentive points the wrong way, what is not counted.

### 3. How it works
The substance. What is the practice? What does it need? What are the steps, conditions and dependencies?

Where the knowledge is practical, use these sub-headings:
- **Context** — where does this apply, and where does it not
- **What to look for** — the signs, readings, indicators
- **What the variations mean** — how to interpret what you find
- **Procedure** — what to do
- **Common failures** — what goes wrong and how to recognise it early

### 4. What cannot be written down
The part that only transfers person to person: judgement, feel, local knowledge, time in the ground. Be honest about how much of the whole this is. A page claiming everything can be learned from a page is not credible.

### 5. Evidence
What already exists. Projects, precedents, published data, historical examples. Link to sources. Where a claim rests on reasoning rather than evidence, say so.

### 6. Unsolved
Open questions. What has not been worked out. What has been tried and failed. This section should never be empty; if it is, the page is not finished.

### 7. Objections
The strongest arguments against, stated fairly, each with an honest response. Where an objection lands and has no good answer, say that.

Weak objections that are easy to knock down do not belong here. Steelman or leave it out.

---

## House rules

**Plain language.** Explain any term the first time it appears. Prefer the short word.

**No hedging by vagueness.** "Some studies suggest" is worse than either citing the study or admitting uncertainty.

**Attribute nothing to a movement.** Write what is true and what is unknown, not what "we believe".

**Length follows content.** A short honest page beats a long padded one.
