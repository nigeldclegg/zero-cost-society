# Resource Allocation

**Domain:** allocation
**Status:** developing

## What it is

Deciding what gets made, what goes where, and who gets what — without price.

The proposal is a division of labour between machines and people. Machines count. People decide what matters. The counting is continuous, open and checkable. The deciding is human, argued in public, and revisable.

That split is the whole idea, and getting it the wrong way round is the failure mode this page exists to prevent.

## Why the current system prevents it

Price is an information system. It aggregates dispersed knowledge about scarcity and demand into a single number, and that number coordinates billions of decisions nobody could coordinate deliberately. This is a real achievement and any serious alternative has to take it seriously.

The problem is what price cannot see.

**It cannot see need, only ability to pay.** Food moves toward money, not toward hunger. This is not a distortion of the mechanism; it is the mechanism.

**It cannot count what nobody owns.** Soil, air, water, ocean, climate, a species. These enter the ledger only when someone can be charged for them, so their depletion registers as free.

**It cannot see the future.** Discounting means a cost fifty years out is nearly zero today. Rational actors therefore make decisions that are individually correct and collectively catastrophic.

**It is not neutral.** A price is an outcome of the existing distribution of wealth. Ask what should be produced and you get an answer weighted by who already has money — which is a political answer wearing a mathematical costume.

## How it works

### Context

Applies within and between communities that have already removed money internally. Says nothing about interfacing with the money economy, which remains unsolved (see `foundations.md`).

Works at the scale of a network of communities. Whether it scales to millions is the central open question of the whole project.

### What to look for

**Continuous inventory, openly readable.** What exists, where, and in what condition. Not a five-year plan but a live picture: which growing systems are short of what, which stores are full, which community has a surplus, what is failing.

**Matching, done by machine.** Given the live picture, working out which surplus should move to which shortfall is arithmetic — a large, fast optimisation problem that machines do better than any committee and at a speed no human process approaches.

**Priorities, set by people.** The machine can find the best allocation *given what you tell it matters*. It cannot tell you what matters. New communities versus established ones. Speed versus resilience. The urgent versus the foundational. These are not calculations, they are values, and they belong to people, argued openly and revisited regularly.

**Every proposal shows its working.** Any allocation the system suggests must be traceable by anyone who cares to look: why this, why here, why now. Openness is not a nicety — it is the only thing preventing the tool from becoming an authority.

### What the variations mean

**"The machine proposed, the people disposed" is the test.** If allocations are ever accepted because the system generated them, the split has failed and something has quietly become a planner.

**Weightings are political and should be argued as such.** The moment priorities are treated as a technical setting rather than a public decision, control has moved from people to whoever configures the system.

**A traceable proposal that nobody can follow is not traceable.** If the reasoning is technically published but incomprehensible, it is opaque in practice. Explicability is a requirement, not a feature.

### Procedure

1. **Measure what actually exists**, continuously and honestly, and make the measurements public within the network.
2. **Let the system propose matchings** — surplus to shortfall, capacity to need.
3. **Set priorities in the open**, by people, on a regular cycle, with the arguments recorded.
4. **Re-run against new priorities** and publish the difference, so everyone can see what the values changed.
5. **Keep a veto.** Somewhere in the structure, a person or rotating group must be able to stop anything that reintroduces price, profit or ownership as the mechanism, however reasonable it sounds. This is `governance.md`'s problem, but allocation is where the pressure will come from.

### Common failures

**Optimising for efficiency.** Efficiency is a value, not a neutral goal, and optimising for it reliably strips the margins — the slack, redundancy and spare capacity that make a system survive a bad year. Feed the margins first.

**Letting the tool set the priorities.** Any system that adjusts its own weightings based on outcomes has begun deciding what matters. This is the single most dangerous failure available here.

**Hiding the reasoning.** Once allocations arrive without explanation, people stop checking, and shortly afterwards the system is an authority.

**Confusing allocation with rationing.** Rationing distributes scarcity. This is a mechanism for distributing sufficiency. If a network is genuinely short of essentials, allocation is not the problem it has.

**Reintroducing price as fairness.** Credits, contribution scores, hours-worked systems. They feel equitable and they restore exactly the behaviour they were meant to prevent.

## What cannot be written down

Judgement about competing legitimate needs.

Two communities need the same thing and both have a real case. No algorithm resolves that, and no page can pre-decide it. What can be transmitted is the *practice* of deciding well: hearing both, deciding in the open, recording why, and revisiting it. What cannot be transmitted is the accumulated sense of when a case is genuine, when a community is quietly failing and not saying so, and when a decision is about to break something socially even though it is numerically correct.

## Evidence

**Non-price allocation exists at scale.** Every large corporation allocates internally without internal markets — a firm the size of a small country moves resources by decision, not price. Coase's theory of the firm is precisely about why this is often *more* efficient than markets. That is not an argument for planning an economy, but it disposes of the claim that non-price allocation cannot work at scale.

**Health systems allocate without price.** Triage, transplant lists and vaccine rollouts distribute scarce, life-critical resources by need and by publicly argued criteria. Contested, imperfect, and functioning.

**Open source coordination.** Large distributed projects allocate the scarcest resource of all — skilled attention — with no prices, no management hierarchy, and no ownership.

**Optimisation is a solved technical problem.** Matching supply to demand across a network is well-understood computationally and used daily in logistics at enormous scale. The technology here is unremarkable; the novelty is entirely in who sets the objective function.

**Commons governance.** Ostrom's design principles for long-lived commons — clear boundaries, participatory rule-making, monitoring, graduated sanctions, and conflict resolution — are the closest thing to an evidence base for the human side of this.

## Unsolved

**Scale.** Everything above is demonstrated at community, firm or network scale. Coordinating essentials for millions of people is not solved, and every historical attempt at economy-wide non-price allocation has failed badly.

**Genuinely scarce goods.** Some things cannot be made abundant: rare minerals, certain medicines, specific land, a particular doctor's time. Allocating these without price is hard, and this repository does not have an answer.

**Who configures the system.** Whoever writes the software makes choices that shape outcomes. Open source is necessary but not sufficient — most people will never read it. This is a real concentration of power and it is unaddressed.

**Gaming.** Any allocation system that responds to reported need creates an incentive to report need. Small communities where everyone knows each other suppress this socially. Larger networks probably do not.

**Speed versus deliberation.** Fast machine matching plus slow human priority-setting works when conditions are stable. What happens in a crisis, when the values also need to change quickly, is not worked out.

## Objections

**"This is central planning, and it killed millions."**
The most serious objection here and it deserves no evasion. Command economies failed catastrophically, both economically and morally. Three differences are claimed: allocation happens between voluntarily networked communities rather than by a state with coercive power; priorities are set openly and locally rather than by a central authority; and nobody is compelled to participate — a community can leave. Whether those differences are sufficient is genuinely unproven, and anyone attracted to this idea should hold the failures of the twentieth century firmly in mind. Any version of this that acquires the power to compel has become the thing it opposed.

**"Hayek was right — no system can gather the knowledge prices carry."**
Hayek's argument was about *information*, and it was correct in 1945: no central body could gather dispersed local knowledge. The claim here is that the informational premise has changed — measurement is now cheap, continuous and distributed, and the knowledge does not have to be centralised to be aggregated. The counter-counter-argument, which stands, is that prices carry *tacit* knowledge about preferences and intentions that no measurement captures. This is unresolved and is the strongest intellectual objection to the project.

**"Somebody still decides, so somebody still has power."**
Correct, and this page does not deny it. The claim is not that power disappears but that it becomes visible, argued, distributed and revocable rather than concentrated in whoever holds capital. That is an improvement, not an escape.

**"Without prices there's no incentive to be efficient."**
Partly true and partly the point. Price-driven efficiency has produced both genuine gains and enormous waste — planned obsolescence, marketing, packaging, and duplicated infrastructure. The response is that transparent measurement provides a different discipline: waste is visible to everyone rather than being someone's margin. Weaker as an incentive, stronger as information.

**"An AI deciding what people get is dystopian."**
It would be, which is why the entire structure of this page is designed to prevent it. The machine proposes and shows its working; people set the priorities and can override any output. If that ever inverts, the objection becomes correct and the system should be shut off.

**"Committees are slow and full of idiots."**
Yes. Every community reports this and it is not solvable, only survivable. The design requirement is that the system tolerate bad committees — that a group can argue about anything at length without being able to reintroduce the money logic or dismantle the commons. You do not design for the absence of foolishness. You design so foolishness cannot reach the important lever.
