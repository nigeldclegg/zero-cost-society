# Knowledge

**Domain:** knowledge
**Status:** developing

## What it is

How practical understanding is kept, improved and passed on when nobody owns it.

Two claims sit underneath this page. First, that knowledge behaves differently from every other resource — giving it away does not diminish it, and the more freely it moves the faster it improves. Second, that most practical knowledge cannot be written down at all, and any system that forgets this produces confident people who cannot do the thing.

Both claims matter. The first is why this project is open. The second is why it will never be enough on its own.

## Why the current system prevents it

**Knowledge is enclosed because enclosure is where the money is.** Patents, copyright, trade secrets and licensing exist to make a non-scarce thing artificially scarce so it can be sold. This is not a side effect; it is the mechanism.

**Research is directed by what pays.** Diseases of the poor go unresearched while minor variations on profitable drugs multiply. Crops that farmers can save seed from are commercially unattractive compared with crops they cannot. The questions asked are shaped by who is paying to have them answered.

**Publicly funded research is privately captured.** Work paid for by taxpayers routinely ends up behind paywalls or in private patents, and the public pays twice.

**Skills are deliberately narrowed.** A worker who understands the whole process is expensive and mobile. A worker who understands one step is cheap and replaceable. Fragmentation is efficient for the employer and corrosive for the knowledge.

**Documentation is treated as overhead.** It produces no revenue and is the first thing cut, which is why so much practical knowledge exists only in individual heads and vanishes with them.

## How it works

### Context

Applies to practical, applicable knowledge: growing, building, repairing, maintaining, treating, deciding. Says less about theoretical research, which needs institutions and instruments beyond community scale.

### What to look for

**The two halves.** Any practical skill divides into a part that can be transmitted in words and images, and a part that only transfers by doing it alongside someone who already can. Roughly half to two-thirds is documentable in most domains; less in soil, more in engineering. Knowing which half you are dealing with is the first skill.

**Documentation carries the pattern, not the judgement.** What to look for, what the readings mean, the procedure, what usually goes wrong. It does not carry knowing that something is wrong before the instruments say so.

**Learners attached to every task.** Not a training programme — the ordinary practice of never doing anything important alone. Knowledge held in one head is a single point of failure, and a community's real resilience is the number of people who can do each thing.

**Open by default.** Everything published, forkable, improvable, with no permission required. The Linux case is the strongest evidence available: released freely, improved collectively, and it outbuilt every proprietary competitor to the point of running most of the connected world.

**Knowledge spreads by contact, like a root system.** It moves between people who work together, and between communities that visit each other, and it does not travel well through documents alone. A network of communities exchanging people is a knowledge system; a network exchanging documents is a library.

### What the variations mean

**Confident and incompetent is the failure signature** of a system that pretends documentation is sufficient. Someone who has read everything and done nothing is more dangerous than someone who knows they do not know.

**If one person is indispensable, the knowledge has already failed**, regardless of how well it is written down.

**A page that claims completeness should be distrusted.** Every genuine practitioner can list what their documentation cannot carry. Its absence indicates someone writing about a thing rather than from it.

### Procedure

1. **Document as you go**, not afterwards. Documentation written from memory is worse and usually never happens.
2. **Write the failures too.** What went wrong, and how it was recognised, is more useful than the clean version.
3. **State the boundary explicitly.** Every document should say what it cannot teach.
4. **Attach a learner to everything.** Ordinary practice, not a programme.
5. **Publish openly**, with a licence that prevents anyone re-enclosing it.
6. **Exchange people between communities**, not just files. This is the part that actually transfers the undocumentable half.

### Common failures

**Believing the documentation is the knowledge.** Produces people who can quote the method and cannot read the situation.

**The single expert.** Comfortable, efficient, and catastrophic the day they leave.

**Documenting only what worked.** The failures are where the learning is, and they are systematically omitted because they are embarrassing.

**Open in name only.** Published but incomprehensible, or technically forkable but practically dependent on one group's servers and goodwill. Openness that cannot be exercised is not openness.

**Letting the tool own the knowledge.** A knowledge system hosted, updated and controlled by one party is enclosure with a friendly interface. If it cannot be forked and run by anyone, it is not free.

## What cannot be written down

The point of the page, so stated plainly.

Roughly a third to a half of any practical skill is judgement acquired by repetition under someone else's eye: knowing when soil is right by looking at it, when a weld has taken, when a group decision is going wrong, when a patient is deteriorating before the numbers move. It is real knowledge, it is reliable, and it is not verbal.

It transfers only by proximity and time. This is why communities are built by contact rather than instruction, why importing a method from documents alone produces fragile imitations, and why this repository is a starting point rather than a manual.

Anyone who tells you a document can replace an apprenticeship is selling something.

## Evidence

**Open source.** Linux, and the wider free software ecosystem, is the strongest available demonstration that complex, valuable production can be organised without ownership or payment, and can outperform proprietary alternatives. It is also honest evidence about the limits: governance disputes, maintainer burnout, and dependence on corporate funding are all real.

**Wikipedia.** Comparable in accuracy to traditional encyclopaedias in multiple studies, built by unpaid contributors, and now foundational infrastructure for the entire internet.

**Open access research** improves citation and use, and the resistance to it is straightforwardly commercial.

**Tacit knowledge** is a well-established field, not a folk belief. Polanyi's "we can know more than we can tell" is the origin; the literature on expertise, apprenticeship and situated learning is extensive and consistent.

**Seed libraries** demonstrate the same principle biologically: varieties kept, grown out, and distributed freely survive; varieties held privately and unused die in the drawer.

**Distributed knowledge in practice.** Communities of practice — from mountain rescue to traditional trades — reliably outperform documented procedures in unpredictable conditions, because the knowledge lives in the group rather than the manual.

## Unsolved

**Verification.** Open knowledge with no gatekeeper means confidently wrong material spreads alongside good. Wikipedia solved this partially with enormous social machinery. A small project has neither the volume nor the editors.

**Maintenance.** Documentation rots. Who updates a page about a method that changed four years ago, when nobody is paid and the original author has moved on?

**Contact at distance.** The undocumentable half needs proximity. How that works between communities on different continents is unresolved, and video is a poor substitute for standing next to someone.

**Theoretical research.** Requires instruments, institutions and long funding horizons that no community can supply. This project has nothing to say about it, which is a real gap.

**Translation.** Practical knowledge is culturally and locally specific, and translating a method also means adapting it to different soil, climate, materials and habits. Machine translation does the words and not the adaptation.

**Assessment.** With no qualifications, how does a community know whether someone actually knows what they claim? Small groups solve this by observation. Larger networks probably cannot.

## Objections

**"Without patents there's no incentive to innovate."**
Contradicted by substantial evidence — open source, academic research, and most historical invention predate or ignore patent protection. What patents demonstrably do is direct innovation toward what can be monopolised. The honest complication is pharmaceuticals, where development costs are genuinely enormous and the alternative funding model is not worked out here.

**"Free knowledge means no quality control."**
Partly true. Open systems produce more errors and correct them faster. Closed systems produce fewer visible errors and hide the ones they have. Neither is obviously better, and this page's Unsolved section concedes the problem rather than claiming it away.

**"You still need experts."**
Yes. Nothing here opposes expertise; it opposes expertise being owned. The proposal is that experts teach continuously rather than holding knowledge as a position, which is how apprenticeship always worked.

**"This project is itself a document claiming to teach what it says can't be taught."**
The sharpest objection available and it lands. This repository can carry the documentable half — the patterns, the procedures, the failures, the objections. It cannot make anyone competent. Every page states this. If this material is ever treated as sufficient, it will produce exactly the confident incompetence described above, and the fault will be with the reader who mistook a map for the ground.

**"Open licences don't stop corporations exploiting the work."**
Under a permissive licence, correct. Under ShareAlike, anything built on it must stay equally free, which is why this project uses it. A company can use the work; it cannot enclose it. That is the most the licence can do, and it is not nothing.
