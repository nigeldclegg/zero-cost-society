# Governance

**Domain:** governance
**Status:** seed

## What it is

How a community decides things, and how a network of communities decides things, without a hierarchy, without a market, and without anyone acquiring the power to reverse the arrangement.

Two jobs, and they are different. Ordinary decisions — what to plant, who does what, how to handle a dispute — need a way of deciding that is participatory without being exhausting. Constitutional protection — preventing the return of price, profit and ownership as the internal mechanism — needs something much harder and much more permanent.

Most attempts at this get the first job roughly right and neglect the second entirely.

## Why the current system prevents it

**Representation at scale removes people from decisions.** Voting every few years for someone who will decide thousands of things on your behalf is a weak form of participation, and everyone involved knows it.

**Money routes around democracy.** Where wealth is concentrated, it purchases outcomes — through lobbying, ownership of media, funding of parties, and simple capacity to wait longer than opponents can. A community that removes money internally removes the main lever by which its decisions could be bought.

**Institutions are captured by the interests they regulate**, reliably and everywhere, because the regulated party has concentrated interest and unlimited patience while the public has diffuse interest and none.

**Good arrangements are reversed by later generations** who did not live through why they were made. Almost nothing in existing governance is designed to be irreversible, because permanent constraint is inconvenient to whoever currently holds power.

## How it works

### Context

Community scale first — dozens to low hundreds of people, where everyone can know everyone. Federation between communities is a second and harder problem. Anything above that is unaddressed here.

### What to look for

**Decisions made by those affected, at the smallest workable scale.** Most decisions do not need a meeting of everyone. They need the people doing the work, deciding as they go.

**Facilitation rather than chairmanship.** Someone whose job is to make sure every voice is heard and the thread is not lost, rotating, without authority over outcomes.

**Consent rather than consensus.** Consensus requires everyone to agree and gives any individual an effective veto over everything, which produces either paralysis or quiet coercion. Consent asks whether anyone has a *reasoned objection* — a much lower bar that still protects the minority.

**A veto that protects one thing only.** Somewhere in the structure, a person or rotating group must be able to block anything that reintroduces price, profit or ownership as the internal mechanism, no matter how reasonable it sounds and no matter how many people vote for it. Not a general veto — a general veto is a dictatorship. One door, guarded permanently.

**Rotation.** Every role that carries influence rotates, on a fixed schedule, whether or not the holder is good at it. Competence is a poor reason to make power permanent.

### What the variations mean

**A decision people accept but nobody argued about is usually a decision that was never real.** Absence of disagreement in a group of any size means either the matter is trivial or dissent is not safe.

**When the same person is always facilitating, the structure has already drifted**, regardless of what the rules say.

**Efficiency arguments are how the money logic returns.** It rarely arrives calling itself profit. It arrives as fairness, or accountability, or "we need to track contributions so it's not unfair on those doing more." That is the moment the veto exists for.

### Procedure

There is no procedure for governing, only for setting it up:

1. **Decide what genuinely needs collective decision** and leave the rest to the people doing the work.
2. **Establish facilitation and rotation from the start**, before anyone has accumulated informal authority — retrofitting rotation onto an established figure is much harder.
3. **Write the constitutional protection early**, while nobody has an interest in weakening it.
4. **Give the protection to someone with no other power**, and rotate it.
5. **Record decisions and the reasons for them**, so that later generations inherit the argument rather than only the rule.

### Common failures

**The founder problem.** The person who started it holds informal authority that no structure removes, and their preferences shape everything long after they have formally stepped back. Rotation on paper does not fix this.

**Meeting exhaustion.** Participatory governance that requires everyone to attend everything produces burnout, then a small group of people who always turn up, which is an unaccountable oligarchy that thinks it is a democracy.

**Consensus as coercion.** Requiring unanimity means the person who holds out is pressured until they stop holding out, which is worse than voting.

**Constitutional drift.** Small, sensible-seeming exceptions accumulate until the protection is nominal. Each individual exception is defensible. That is what makes it work.

**Nobody being able to say no.** Structures designed entirely for agreement have no mechanism for refusal, and are therefore defenceless against a proposal that everyone feels vaguely uneasy about but nobody can formally block.

## What cannot be written down

Almost all of it.

Knowing when a meeting has stopped being a discussion and become a pressure exercise. Sensing that someone has not spoken and needs drawing in. Recognising when a technically correct decision is about to break something socially. Understanding a specific group's history well enough to know which arguments are about the topic and which are about something from four years ago.

This is why governance transfers badly between communities and why importing another group's constitution rarely works. The rules are the small part.

## Evidence

**Ostrom's design principles** remain the strongest evidence base: long-lived commons across centuries and continents share identifiable features — clear boundaries, rules made by those affected, monitoring by the participants, graduated sanctions, accessible conflict resolution, and recognised rights to organise. This is empirical, Nobel-recognised, and directly applicable.

**Sociocracy and consent-based methods** are documented and practised in numerous organisations, with published methods and honest accounts of failure.

**Cooperative governance** at large scale — Mondragón being the most studied — demonstrates that non-hierarchical structures survive commercial pressure for decades, while also demonstrating the compromises that pressure produces.

**Open source project governance** offers the closest analogue to federation: independent projects, no central authority, forking as the ultimate check on bad leadership.

**Intentional community failures** are as instructive as successes and better documented than most people assume. The Foundation for Intentional Community and academic literature on communal longevity both indicate that governance failure, not economic failure, is the commonest cause of collapse.

## Unsolved

**Federation.** How communities decide things *between* them, with no central authority, is barely addressed here and is the immediate practical gap.

**Scale.** Everything above assumes people can know each other. Nothing here works at a million people and pretending otherwise would be dishonest.

**Exclusion.** Whether a community can refuse someone, on what grounds, and who decides. Unavoidable and almost entirely unexamined in this repository.

**Enforcement.** What happens when someone persistently breaks an agreement and social pressure fails. Every real community faces this and the answers are uncomfortable.

**Succession of protection.** The veto works while its holders remember why it exists. Three generations on, when nobody has lived under money, what stops it being repealed as an anachronism?

**Emergency.** Deliberative governance is slow. Crises are fast. Temporary concentrations of authority are how permanent ones begin.

## Objections

**"Someone always ends up in charge."**
Frequently true, and rotation plus transparency mitigate rather than eliminate it. The claim is not that informal power vanishes but that it can be made visible and temporary. Founders are the hardest case and no structure fully solves them.

**"This only works with people who already agree."**
Largely true, and a real limit. Voluntary communities self-select for people sympathetic to their premises. Governance among people who fundamentally disagree about the premises is a different and harder problem, and this page does not address it.

**"Direct democracy is mob rule."**
Which is exactly why the constitutional protection exists. A majority cannot reintroduce the money logic. That is deliberately undemocratic in the narrow sense, on the grounds that some things should not be available to a vote — the same reasoning behind any constitution.

**"A permanent veto is just a different kind of tyranny."**
The strongest objection here. A single unelected person able to block decisions is a concentration of power, and calling it constitutional does not change that. The defence is that it blocks precisely one thing, cannot compel anything, and rotates. Whether that is enough is not settled, and anyone designing this should treat the objection as live.

**"Meetings are unbearable."**
Yes. This is the most common complaint from people who have actually lived it, and it is a design problem, not a moral failing. Any version of this that requires people to spend their evenings in rooms deciding things will lose exactly the people it most needs.

**"You cannot bind future generations."**
Legally and practically contestable, and philosophically uncomfortable. The honest response is that every constitution attempts it, that all such attempts eventually fail, and that the aim is to make reversal slow and deliberate rather than impossible.
